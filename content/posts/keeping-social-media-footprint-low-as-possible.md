Here are some perspectives that I share when people ask us why: 1). [In France, parents could face finest up to EUR 45.000 for publishing photos of their children online without permission.](https://www.theguardian.com/sustainable-business/2016/may/08/children-sue-parents-facebook-post-baby-photos-privacy). While this might feel pretty extreme, I appreciate the idea of having to have a sort of consent before exposing your child to the world. I certainly would not have liked that when I was younger. 2). [Facebook looks for your face in photos you're not tagged in](https://www.theverge.com/2017/12/19/16794660/facebook-facial-recognition-tagging-photos). There is more material to find around this and the technology is already a lot more advanced than the article describes. Facebook builds "shadow profiles" for people that don't have a Facebook account to improve their social network graph. As soon as such a person eventually signs up, they apply the already harvested knowledge to that profile and close the gaps. While it might sound useful, they are processing personal data here without explicit consent. 3). [Today's internet pretty much ignores this, but people have a right to be forgotten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_to_be_forgotten) . How will you as a parent deal with a depressed and anxious teenager that is bothered by all the public material online that might not fit their current identity anymore? How do you know you'll be able to clean up if your child wants that? I feel we have a responsibility here and would like my child to be in control. 4). [China surveillance tech can find you in a 60.000 people crowd](https://www.bbc.com/news/world-asia-china-43751276). Think about this: if facial recognition is this accurate in 2018, how will it be in 2028? Any artifact posted online could be feeding these kinds of technology and you might have impeded your child's dream of being a free person that could go where ever they want. 5). [Facebook is a very smart business that puts revenue first.](https://www.forbes.com/sites/parmyolson/2018/09/26/exclusive-whatsapp-cofounder-brian-acton-gives-the-inside-story-on-deletefacebook-and-why-he-left-850-million-behind/) They can never be trusted with handling user data in an ethical way. This is how we do it: We send pictures through iMessage and keep a private Google Photos album shared with grandparents. We don't enable facial recognition features and send family actual prints of nice photos. While this might not be perfect, it at least allows us to stay aware of what we share and how.
